Title: Modeling Physical Phenomenon: A derivation and exploration of Laplacian growth.
Speaker: Nathan Hayford.
Date: Thursday, August $29^{th}$
Time: 3:30pm
Place: CMC 109
Abstract: Often the most interesting mathematical problems are physically motivated: Laplacian growth is no exception. However, one may wonder how such models are derived, i.e., what is their physical relevance, what constitutes a good balance between an easily solvable problem and a physically realistic one, among other questions. In this talk, I plan on deriving Laplacian growth from first principles physics, and explain the original motivations of the equation (in short, modeling a 2D fluid domain’s expansion/retraction as fluid is pumped in/out from above). I will briefly discuss the integrable structure of Laplacian growth, and how it leads to exact solutions of the equation; finally, if time permits, I will mention another modern application of Laplacian growth in condensed matter theory: the integer quantum Hall effect.\

Title: Banach Algebra Approach to Wiener’s Theorem.
Speaker: Raymond Centner.
Date: Thursday, August $15^{th}$
Time: 2:00pm
Place: CMC 108
Abstract: The Banach algebra setting can be very natural and useful in harmonic analysis. In 1932, Norbert Wiener proved that the space of absolutely convergent Fourier series is inverse closed, i.e., if f is a non-vanishing continuous function on the circle with an absolutely convergent Fourier series, then 1/f has an absolutely convergent Fourier series. Roughly ten years later, Israel Gelfand proved this result using the theory of Banach algebras. The purpose of this talk is to present this theorem in the context of Banach algebras, shedding light on the usefulness of abstraction in classical analysis.\

Title: What is ... the inverse scattering transform?
Speaker: Fudong Wang.
Date: Thursday, August $8^{th}$
Time: 3:30pm
Place: CMC 108
Abstract: The story begins after the paper about a method for solving the KdV equation by Gardner, Greene, Kruskal, and Miura (GGKM) at 1967. That’s a first example of using IST to solve a nonlinear integrable equation. In 1968, Lax found an operator way to generalize GGKM’s method, which we now called “Lax pair”. In 1971, Zakharov and Shabat use Lax’s method to solve the NLS equation. In 1974, Ablowitz, Kaup, Newell, and Segur (AKNS) compare GGKM, Lax and ZS’s method in nonlinear PDEs with the Fourier transform in the linear PDEs and the term “inverse scattering transform” was born. In this talk, we will briefly discuss how the inverse scattering transform works on solving the NLS equation. If time allows, we will also talk about how to use IST to generate the beautiful “soliton” wave solution.\

Title: A Theorem of Radó Type for the p-Laplace equation in the Heisenberg group
Speaker: Zachary Forrest.
Date: Wednesday, August $8^{th}$
Time: 11:00am
Place: CMC 108
Abstract: It is known that in Euclidean spaces, the p-Laplace equation possesses a Radó-type result: That is, given a domain $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ and a fixed exponent $1<\texttt{p}<\infty$, a function $u \in C^1(\Omega)$ which is a viscosity solution to the $\texttt{p}$- Laplace equation $$-\Delta_\texttt{p}u=0 ,,,, (1)$$ in the set is also viscosity solution solution to Equation (1) in $\Omega$. This result was proven in a paper (2005) of Juutinen and Lindqvist via the use of the Implicit Function Theorem, and motivated by this approach and the works of Franchi-Serapioni-Serra Cassano (2007) & Bieske (2006) we prove a removability result for $\texttt{p}$-harmonic functions in the first Heisenberg group $\mathbb{H}_1$.

Title: A Survey of the Polynomial Method.
Speaker: Austin Dukes.
Date: Thursday, August $1^{st}$
Time: 3:30pm
Place: CMC 13
Abstract: Recently, we have seen a surge of progress on many long-standing problems from various fields using methods based on ... polynomials? Based on relatively simple combinatorial arguments and dimension-counting from linear algebra, these polynomial methods were not initially perceived to be especially insightful. However, applying polynomials has shed light on subtle yet essential structure in many problems spanning many areas, and these methods are currently an exciting and active area of research. In this talk, we will discuss the Polynomial Method as well as give various examples of its use in fields such as incidence geometry, harmonic analysis, and computer science.\

Title: Geometric group theory as a mathematical melting pot.
Speaker: Lina Fajardo Gomez.
Date: Thursday, July $18^{th}$
Time: 3:30pm
Place: CMC 13
Abstract: Geometric group theory is an area that studies discrete groups through the connections between algebraic properties of the groups and the topological and geometric properties of the spaces they act on. It is a relatively new area of study that remains very active (as demonstrated by the many conferences in the field and programs that focus on it). This seminar talk is intended as a broad introduction to geometric group theory, with examples of how it relates to different areas of mathematics, and a motivation for its study.\

Title: Some fundamental formulas on the Cauchy-type integrals.
Speaker: Fudong Wang.
Date: Thursday, July $11^{th}$
Time: 3:30pm
Place: CMC 13
Abstract: In the theory of boundary value problems of analytic functions, Cauchy-type integrals play a fundamental role. There are several fundamental formulas in the field, such like Sokhotsky-Plemelj formula, Poincaré-Bertrand formula, Plemelj-Privalov theorem, and Marcel Riesz’s inequality on the Hilbert transform. In this seminar talk, we will start from the beginning to prove some of the mentioned theorems. Those theorems are frequently applied in the theory of Riemann-Hilbert problem, Random matrices and integrable systems.\

Title: Computing the Regulator of a Real Quadratic Order Using p-Adic Methods.
Speaker: Will Youmans.
Date: Thursday, June $27^{th}$
Time: 3:30pm
Place: CMC 13
Abstract: The regulator of a real quadratic order is defined as the logarithm of the generator of the unit group (called the fundamental unit). This value is closely tied to the class number and describes the "density" of the units. The coefficients of the fundamental unit can become very large, so rather than directly compute the regulator from the fundamental unit itself we typically use a power product representation of units derived from a generating set of relations for the class group. Existing methods for computing the regulator from this data suffer from a variable loss of precision which can be difficult to control. We propose a p-adic method which simplifies the management of the precision throughout the computation.\

Title: What Can Random Matrices Do For You? Speaker: Nathan Hayford.
Date: Thursday, June $20^{th}$
Time: 3:30pm
Place: CMC 13
Abstract: Random matrix theory has been an indispensable tool for mathematical physicists for the past few decades, but one may wonder how relevant it is outside of this field. In this talk, I plan on explaining the basics of random matrices, and how they have been used in fields such as knot theory, graph combinatorics, number theory, biology, quantum gravity, integrable systems, orthogonal polynomials, and... you get the point. If time permits (and depending on the audience), I’ve also prepared some more detailed examples of how random matrix theory is used in each of these contexts. I’ll try and share as many of these as I can in the allotted time.\
