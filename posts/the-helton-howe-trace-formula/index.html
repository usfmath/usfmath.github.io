The Helton Howe Trace Formula

Nathan Hayford
May 1, 2021

We present a proof of a simple version of the Helton-Howe trace formula (for a more general version of this result, one should consult the original paper). Consider the Hardy space $H^2(\mathbb{D})$. There is a natural inclusion of $H^2(\mathbb{D})$ into $L^2(\mathbb{T})$, given by the restriction $f\mapsto f\vert_{\mathbb{T}}$. Let $\mathbb{P}_{H^2} : L^2(\mathbb{T}) \to H^2(\mathbb{D})$ denote the orthogonal projection of $L^2(\mathbb{T})$ into $H^2(\mathbb{D})$. Now, given $P \in \mathbb{C}[z,z^{-1}]$, we define the Toeplitz operator with principal symbol $P$ by $$T_P f(z) = \mathbb{P}_{H^2} \left[ P(z)f(z)\right],$$ for $f \in H^2(\mathbb{D})$. It is clear that the commutator of any two such operators operators is trace-class (it is even of finite rank). The Helton-Howe formula tells us that, given $P,Q \in \mathbb{C}[z,z^{-1}]$, $$\text{tr } [T_P, T_Q] = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{|z| = 1} P(z) Q'(z) dz = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{\mathbb{D}} dP \wedge dQ$$ The last equality is immediate from Stokes' theorem; the interesting equality is the first. Let $T_z$ denote the Toeplitz operator of multiplication by $z$ (this is just the shift operator in the Hardy space), and $T^*_z$ its adjoint (note that $T^*_z = T_{1/z}$). If $P = \sum_k P_k z^k \in \mathbb{C}[z,z^{-1}]$, then its corresponding Toeplitz operator may be written as $$T_P = \sum_{k\geq 0} P_k T_z^k + \sum_{k<0} P_k {T_z^{*}}^k.$$ Clearly, ${T_z^*}^n{T_z}^n = I$; on the other hand, ${T_z}^n{T_z^*}^n = I - \mathbb{P}_n$, where $\mathbb{P}_n$ is the orthogonal projector onto the subspace $E_n:=\text{span} {1,z, …,z^{n-1}}$. This observation allows us to compute that $\text{tr }[{T_z^*}^n,{T_z}^n] = \text{tr } \mathbb{P}_n = n$. Now, if $n \neq m$, one finds by direct computation that $\text{tr }[{T_z^*}^m,{T_z}^n] = 0$. Thus, $\text{tr }[{T_z^*}^m,{T_z}^n] = n\delta_{n,m}$. If $P, Q \in \mathbb{C}[z,z^{-1}]$, and $P = \sum_k P_k z^k$, $Q = \sum_k Q_k z^k$, then $$= \sum_{n,m \geq 0} (P_{-n}Q_m - Q_{-n}P_m) [{T_z^*}^n,{T_z}^m];$$ taking traces yields $$\begin{aligned} \text{tr } [T_P,T_Q] &= \sum_{n,m \geq 0} (P_{-n}Q_m - Q_{-n}P_m) \text{tr } [{T_z^*}^n,{T_z}^m] \\\
&= \sum_{n,m \geq 0} (P_{-n}Q_m - Q_{-n}P_m) n\delta_{n,m} = \sum_{n\geq 0} n(P_{-n}Q_n - Q_{-n}P_n)\\\
&= \sum_{n}n P_{-n}Q_n. \end{aligned}$$ On the other hand, by residue theorem, $$\begin{aligned} \frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{|z| = 1} P(z) Q'(z) dz &= \frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{|z| = 1} \sum_{\ell}\left(\sum_n nP_{\ell - n} Q_n \right) z^{\ell-1} dz\\\
&= \sum_{\ell}\left(\sum_n nP_{\ell - n} Q_n \right) \delta_{\ell,0}\\\
&= \sum_{n}n P_{-n}Q_n. \end{aligned}$$ Comparing the results of these two computations concludes the proof.
